Pepsi vs. generic cola in one measurement
Q: I am hosting a party and have 10 guests. Each guest, as per my request, is bringing 1000 bottles of Pepsi. However, at least one of my friends is dishonest, and is instead bringing 1000 bottles of generic cola. The bottles are identical in every way except that one bottle of Pepsi weights 10 units, whereas one bottle of generic cola weights 9 units. Fortunately, I have a huge scale, and can get the weight of as many bottles as I want, but it will only work for one measurement. How can I determine exactly who brought generic cola?

A: Label the guests \(g_0, g_1,...,g_9\). Take \(2^i\) bottles from guest \(i\) and weigh these 1023 bottles. Say these bottles weigh \(w\) units: We write \(10230-w\) in binary, and if the \(i\)th digit (from right to left) is a 1, guest \(i\) brought 1000 bottles of generic cola instead of Pepsi.

S: I was asked this question in an interview and it took me a while to figure this out. At first I thought this was the well-known King's Wine Problem which is roughly as follows: A King is hosting a party in 24 hours, and is gifted 1000 barrels of wine. The King knows exactly one of them is poisoned, and he wants to figure out which barrel is poisoned before the party. The poison takes exactly 23 hours to have an effect. The King has 10 slaves that he intends to use to drink some wine from barrels, and can see who dies. How does he figure out which is the bad barrel before the party starts?

The solution to the King's Wine Problem is to have slave \(i\) drink from barrel \(j\) if \(j\) written in binary has a 1 in the \(i\)th digit (from right to left), and this works because the slaves who die the next day (and those who don't) will spell out the binary number of which barrel is the bad one. For example, suppose barrel #21, which is 0000010101 in binary, is the bad barrel—then the king would have forced slaves 1, 3, and 5 drink that barrel, and only slaves 1, 3, and 5 would die, which would spell out 25. Since we have 10 slaves, we can test up to 1023 barrels of wine, since 1111111111 in binary is 1023.

I don't think I would have gotten this question correct without knowing this solution first, which is why I included it. (A key to doing well on these is knowing most of the common tricks beforehand). The thinking behind my solution is similar—if I have each guest put a different number of their bottles on the scale, I can identify exactly who is cheating me from Pepsi. Notice that I get 1 bottle from \(g_0\) and 512 bottles from \(g_9\), which makes 1023 bottles total, since \(1 + 2 + 4 + ... + 2^{k} = 2^{k+1}-1\). So, I would hope that they weigh 10230 units altogether, but if it weighs 10227 units, for example, I would know that exactly \(g_0\), who put in 1 bottle, and \(g_1\), who put in 2 bottles, were the generic-cola perpetrators. Thus, every number from 9207 (everyone brought generic cola) to 10230 can be mapped to dishonest guests. I should get new friends, and get dental insurance.
Probability that newborn is a boy
Q: In a hospital's nursery room, there are two boys and a number of girls. A baby is born and put in the nursery, and then a baby is randomly selected from the nursery: It's a boy. What is the probability that the newborn is a boy? Hint: Bayes' Theorem.

A: \(3/5\)

S: As the hint suggests, this is a conditional probability question. Let \(B\) be the event that the newborn is a boy. Let \(C_B\) be the event that the randomly chosen baby is a boy. We are interested in \( P(B | C_B) \). Let \(g\) be the number of girls in the nursery room before the new baby is born. By applying Bayes' Theorem, we get: $$ \begin{eqnarray} P(B | C_B) & = & \frac{P(C_B | B) P(B)}{P(C_B)} \\ & = & \frac{(\frac{3}{3+g})(\frac{1}{2})}{P(C_B)} \end{eqnarray} $$ Then we can get \(C_B\) as follows: $$ \begin{eqnarray} P(C_B) & = & P(C_B | B)P(B) + P(C_B | \neg B)P(\neg B) \\ & = & \left(\frac{3}{3+g}\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) + \left(\frac{2}{3+g}\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) \\ & = & \frac{5}{6 + 2g} \end{eqnarray} $$ And substituting it in, we get: $$ P(B | C_B) = \left(\frac{6+2g}{5}\right)\left(\frac{3}{6+2g}\right) = 3/5 $$
Envelopes of money game
This is from Max Dama on Automated Trading.

Q: There are closed envelopes with $2, $4, $8, $16, $32, and $64 lined up in sorted order in front of you. Two envelopes next to each other are picked up and shuffled. One is given to you and one is given to your friend. You and your friend then open your own envelopes, look inside, and then decide whether or not to offer to trade. If you both agree to trade, then you will swap. Assume you open your envelope and see $16. Should you offer to trade?

A: No.

S: If we see $16, our friend has either $32 or $8. If he has $32, he will not accept our trade—from his point of view, we either have $64, in which case we obviously wouldn't offer to trade, or $16, in which case he doesn't want to trade. So, if we offered to trade, he would know we must have $16. Therefore, our friend might only offer to trade if he had $8 in his envelope, so we should not trade our $16 (although, he wouldn't offer to trade anyways—think about it).
1 vs 1 die roll game
This is from Max Dama on Automated Trading.

Q: Play a 1 vs 1 game. I roll a 6 sided die, if I get a 1 I win, if not you roll. If you get a 6 you win, otherwise I go again... What are my chances of winning and what are yours?

A: The first roller's probability of winning is \(6/11\). The second roller's probability of winning is \(5/11\).

S: Let \(F\) be the event that the first roller wins this game. Then we have: $$ P(F) = \frac{1}{6} + \left(\frac{5}{6}\right)^2 P(F) $$ Solving for \(P(F)\), we get \(P(F) = 6/11\). So \(P( \neg F) = 5/11\)

This is because the first roller can win the game immediately, which happens with probability \(1/6\), or both the first roller and the second roller roll duds with probability \((5/6)^2\), and then the first roller wins with the same initial probability \(P(F)\).
